Knishes - the Authentic, Fabulous, East-Coast version

Ingredients
  • 7 baking potatoes, peeled and cubed
  • 1/4 cup vegetable oil
  • 2 onions, chopped
  • 1 teaspoon ground black pepper
  • 2 teaspoons salt
  • 1 teaspoon minced garlic
  • 2 eggs
  • 1 cup warm water
  • 4 cups all-purpose flour
  • 2 egg yolks
  • 2 tablespoons milk

There are places where you can buy knishes in San Francisco, including Miller's East Coast Deli and Shorty Goldestein's.

History of the Knish

If you're from New York, chances are you've had a knish before, and know that's an ultimate form of Jewish soul food. When did America come to enjoy delicious knishes? Sometime around 1900, Eastern European immigrants brought knishes to North America, and in particular, New York. They were especially prominent on the Lower East Side of Manhattan and the boardwalks of Brooklyn. The aforementioned immigrants usually sold it from pushcarts and baskets. "Knish" is actually a Yiddish word derived from "knysh" in Ukranian. Not totally sure what it translates to, probably "pure deliciousness." Wikipedia History

What's a Knish?

CARB HEAVEN. A knish is a pillow of dough, usually stuffed with oniony mashed potatoes. They can have sweet or savory fillings, including potato, mushroom, spinach, sweet cheese, or fruit fillings. For some reason, the square ones are generally potato with a crispy exterior. Thekitchn's Guide to Knishes

Directions

  1. Place the potatoes into a large pot and cover with salted water. Bring to a boil over high heat, then reduce heat to medium-low, cover, and simmer until tender, about 20 minutes. Drain and allow to steam dry for a minute or two. Add the bell pepper, sauté for 5-7 minutes over medium until softened.
  2. Heat 1/4 cup of vegetable oil in a skillet over medium heat. Stir in the onion; cook and stir until the onion has softened and turned translucent, about 5 minutes. Combine potatoes and onions in a large bowl; season with 2 teaspoons of salt, pepper, sugar, and garlic. Mash until smooth; set aside.
  3. Preheat an oven to 375 degrees F (190 degrees C). Line a baking sheet with parchment paper.
  4. Beat eggs, 1/2 cup of vegetable oil, warm water, and 1 teaspoon of salt. Gradually stir in flour. When the dough has pulled together, turn it out onto a lightly floured surface and knead until smooth and elastic, about 8 minutes.
  5. Divide the dough into thirds and roll each piece out to about 9x13-inches. Slice the rolled dough in half lengthwise, then spread the potato mixture down the center of each strip. Roll each strip around the filling and gently press to seal. Repeat with the remaining dough and filling to end with six logs.
  6. Cut each log into 1-inch slices and place cut side down on the prepared baking sheet. Gently press the edges of the dough toward the center of the potato mixture to form a bun. Beat egg yolks and milk together in a small bowl. Brush each knish with the the egg yolk mixture.
  7. Bake in the preheated oven until golden brown, 35 to 45 minutes.

How do I eat it?

For non-New Yorkers, a knish may not be a self explanatory snack. Do you eat it cold? Hot? Dipped in sauce? The answer's simple: hot and plain! If it's not already served piping hot, heat that ish up and place directly into your mouth.
